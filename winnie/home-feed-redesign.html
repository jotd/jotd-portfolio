Home Feed Redesign

Overview

Winnie is a marketplace for child care, kids activities and backed by a trusted community of parents and providers.

project

Winnie's home feed needed a redesign to surface local data. Based on support tickets and App Store Reviews, it was clear users were confused by seeeing a feed consisting entirely of parenting discussions. Users expected to find local child care and kids activities immediately after opening the app but the feed was originally designed for content contribution and engagement.

Although Winnie was making a big investment to collect local data across different markets, it was not being surfaced in the home feed. This created a negative experience for new users who were presented with a feed of discussions instead of affordances for local search and discovery. Monetization was also a consideration because child care providers paid to be promoted in search results. So additional advertising inventory could be created in the home feed.

My hypothesis was that If I added affordances for local search and discovery to the home feed then I could increase the number of place searches as well as place detail views. As a result, the home feed experience would match user expectations and paying partners could reach more potential customers.

results

72% vs. 42% of new users completed a local search in their first app session. This +30% lift reflected that new users could search and discover local places faster, right from the home feed.

The number of place detail views did not meaningfully increase because the CTR from search results to place detail stayed flat. So even though users were creating more searches, they were not seeing results that matched their intent.

Survey Data

To inform my design decisions, I surveyed a random sample of 1K parents who had used the iOS app in the past three months.

key insights

A majority of users said their goal in using Winnie was to find local child care (8%) and activities (52%) which made sense since Winnie's maine value prop is local data.

Only 18% of users said they wanted advice or support from other parents. This made me realize that I could push down UGC in the home feed since most users didn't find value in it.

In terms of how users wanted to acommplish this goal, a majority said they wanted to use search queries (14%) and explore a map (26%) to see a ranked list of results. This meant that shortcuts to create search queries and explore a map could be added above the home feed.

Approach

My strategy for creating a new home feed architecture revolved around intent, browse and nurture.

At the top of feed, a user would be able to quickly create a search based on intent. Below search affordances, a user could browse local providers for child care and kids activities. The rest of feed would show parenting discussions and static resources.

First Iteration

I started off by exploring different ways to display search shortcut buttons which served two purposes. Not only could users see search results faster but they would also understand the variety of local data that was available for search.

Displaying "filtered" search buttons such as Montessori didn't make sense because we don't have strong data collection in every major market. As a result, lots of users would end up in an undesirable low data or empty state.

Second Iteration

Then I delved into different ways to help users discover local places and also manage places that they had previously viewed and saved. However due to backend engineering constraints, it was out of scope to show viewed places.

To increase CTR from feed to place detail, I proposed personalizing the places shown in the segmented control based on their search intent. During onboarding, users are shown a simple form where they can use checkboxes to state whether they are looking for daycares, preschools, kids activitie or drop-in care. The responses to this question were then used to populate the segmented control. The default state would show a mix of child care and kids activities.

Shipped Design

The Nearby button launches a full screen map view with a carousel of places. As a user scrolls through the carousel, the associated map pin scales up to provide feedback. In terms of visual treatment, the active map pin would have a white icon against a colored background for contrast against inactive map pins. The point of each map pin would denote the latitutde and longitude.

The other search buttons would trigger a search with results based on the category such as Daycares, Preschools, Activities etc. Below the search shortcuts, I placed a segmented control allowing the user to toggle between featured and viewed places. The default state for the featured places section would be local child care and kids activities based on the user's device location. Some randomness and a quality filter (description, reviews) was added so this section felt curated to users. If the device location can't be detected then the user's home location captured in onboarding would be used. If no places are found, the radius would be expanded until the max limit is reached. If no places are found after reaching the max radius, then the segmented control would be hidden altogether.

Furthermore, the places that populate the carousel would be personalized based on onboarding. If a user has no saved places, there would be a prompt urging the user to create a search to discover local places. Tapping on the prompt would take the user to the search tab.
